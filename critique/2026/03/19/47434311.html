We Have Learned Nothing (colossus.com)

31 points by lukestevens 4 hours ago  | 8 comments critique  | original on HN

Summary

The article critiques the prevailing methods and theories of startup entrepreneurship, arguing that despite widespread adoption of frameworks like Lean Startup and customer development, startup survival rates have not improved over the past 30 years. It suggests that the current 'science' of entrepreneurship is flawed or incomplete and proposes embracing a dynamic, adaptive approach inspired by the Red Queen hypothesis to foster genuine innovation and competitive advantage.

Main Points & Arguments

  • Traditional startup advice was inadequate for high-growth startups, leading to the rise of new methods like customer development, Lean Startup, and design thinking.
  • Despite these new methods being widely taught and adopted, U.S. startup survival rates have remained flat for decades, indicating no real improvement.
  • The current entrepreneurship frameworks lack rigorous scientific testing and may function more as pseudoscience due to incentives that discourage falsification.
  • Theories may be too obvious, widely adopted, or self-defeating because uniform application leads to competitive convergence and no relative advantage.
  • A true science of entrepreneurship should focus on generating new methods and embrace variability and rule-breaking rather than fixed formulas.
  • The Red Queen hypothesis illustrates that startups must continuously innovate and differentiate to survive, as doing what everyone else does yields no advantage.

Truthfulness Assessment

Reliable

The article's claims about the stagnation of startup survival rates are supported by referenced U.S. government data from the Bureau of Labor Statistics and venture capital funding analyses, which show no significant improvement in startup longevity or success despite the popularity of new entrepreneurial methods. The critique of the lack of rigorous scientific testing in entrepreneurship aligns with academic observations about challenges in measuring startup success and the incentives in the field. The discussion of the Red Queen hypothesis and its application to business strategy is a recognized concept in evolutionary economics and competitive strategy literature. Overall, the article presents a well-supported and nuanced critique based on available data and established theories, though some interpretations about the effectiveness of specific methods may be subject to debate within the entrepreneurship community.

Additional Considerations

  • The article focuses primarily on survival rates and funding rounds as measures of startup success, which may not capture other dimensions such as profitability, innovation impact, or founder satisfaction.
  • External factors like economic cycles, technological shifts, and changes in venture capital dynamics also influence startup outcomes and may confound assessments of method effectiveness.
  • The article does not deeply explore successful case studies where the new methods may have contributed positively, potentially overlooking heterogeneity in outcomes.
  • The challenge of conducting rigorous, large-scale randomized controlled trials in entrepreneurship is significant and may limit definitive conclusions about method efficacy.
  • The Red Queen hypothesis applies under relatively stable environmental conditions; radical market or technological disruptions might alter competitive dynamics in ways not fully addressed.
