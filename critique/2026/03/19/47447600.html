Bombarding gamblers with offers greatly increases betting and gambling harm (bristol.ac.uk)

Summary

A study led by Central Queensland University and the University of Bristol found that gamblers who receive regular direct marketing offers place more bets, spend more money, and experience greater gambling-related harms compared to those who opt out. The research provides evidence of a causal link between exposure to gambling marketing and increased gambling harm, supporting calls for tighter regulations.

Main Points & Arguments

  • Gamblers receiving direct marketing offers placed 23% more bets and spent 39% more money than those who opted out.
  • Participants exposed to marketing reported 67% more short-term gambling harms such as distress.
  • The study was a randomized controlled trial involving 227 regular sports and race bettors in Australia.
  • The findings challenge the UK Government's 2023 position that there was no causal link between marketing and gambling harm.
  • Personal testimony from a recovered gambler highlights the manipulative nature of personalized gambling marketing.
  • Researchers and advocacy groups call for tighter restrictions or bans on direct gambling marketing to protect consumers.
