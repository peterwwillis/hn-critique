Summary

The article explains the Content Scrambling System (CSS) used in DVDs to prevent unauthorized copying by encrypting video data and requiring licensed DVD players to decrypt it. It discusses the cracking of CSS by the DeCSS software, legal battles surrounding it, and the subsequent development of stronger content protection systems like CPPM.

Main Points & Arguments

  • CSS was developed in 1996 as a cryptosystem to protect DVDs from illegal copying by encrypting video data and requiring licensed DVD players to decrypt it.
  • DVD players receive secret keys from the DVD Copy Control Association (DVD CCA) to decrypt the content, preventing unauthorized copying.
  • DeCSS, released in 1999 by Jon Johansen and others, cracked CSS to enable DVD playback on Linux systems, leading to legal challenges.
  • Despite legal efforts by the DVD CCA and MPAA, DeCSS could not be effectively suppressed and was ruled by some courts as protected free speech.
  • CSS has inherent weaknesses such as a small key space and reliance on secret algorithms, leading to its eventual compromise.
  • The industry responded by developing stronger encryption systems like CPPM using the Cryptomeria Cipher (C2).
  • CSS uses a multi-stage decryption process involving disc keys, title keys, and player keys, and employs Linear Feedback Shift Registers (LFSRs) for encryption.
