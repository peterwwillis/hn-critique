Summary

The article presents an investigative data journalism project analyzing fixable waste in the US healthcare system, identifying nearly $100 billion in potential savings annually by addressing pricing inefficiencies in drugs and hospital procedures. It compares US healthcare spending and outcomes with peer nations and proposes specific policy fixes based on primary federal and international data sources.

Main Points & Arguments

  • The US spends about $14,570 per person on healthcare, significantly more than Japan's $5,790, despite Japan having the highest life expectancy in the OECD.
  • Medicare pays prescription prices for over-the-counter drugs, leading to an estimated $0.6 billion in unnecessary spending annually.
  • The US pays 7 to 25 times more than peer nations for the same brand-name drugs, with potential savings of $25 billion through international reference pricing.
  • Commercial insurers pay 254% of Medicare rates for identical hospital procedures, with a potential $73 billion annual saving by capping payments at 200% of Medicare rates.
  • The project uses primary data sources like CMS cost reports, RAND studies, OECD statistics, and provides open-source reproducible analysis.
