Summary

A study from Cornell University published in Personality and Individual Differences found that workers who are most impressed by corporate jargon and 'corporate bullshit' tend to make poorer business decisions. The research developed a 'Corporate Bullshit Receptivity Scale' and showed that susceptibility to such language correlates with lower analytical thinking but higher perceptions of leadership charisma and job satisfaction.

Main Points & Arguments

  • Corporate bullshit is defined as confusing, semantically empty corporate jargon intended to impress or persuade.
  • Workers who are most receptive to corporate bullshit scored lower on analytical thinking and decision-making tasks.
  • The study used a corporate bullshit generator and real corporate quotes to test 1,000 office workers' perceptions and decision-making.
  • A new scale, the Corporate Bullshit Receptivity Scale, was developed to measure susceptibility to corporate jargon.
  • Susceptible workers rated their supervisors as more charismatic and visionary and reported higher job satisfaction.
  • The findings apply across highly educated workers, indicating susceptibility is not limited to intelligence levels.
  • Examples cited include Pepsi's 2009 rebranding and Elizabeth Holmes' use of corporate jargon to mislead investors.
