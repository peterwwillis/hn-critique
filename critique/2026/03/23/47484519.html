"Collaboration" Is Bullshit (joanwestenberg.com)

46 points by mitchbob 3 hours ago  | 12 comments critique  | original on HN

Summary

The article critiques the modern obsession with collaboration in organizations, arguing that it often leads to diffusion of responsibility and reduced individual accountability. It highlights historical and organizational research showing that a small fraction of individuals do most of the work, while the rest provide minimal effort, and suggests that true ownership and responsibility are essential for productivity.

Main Points & Arguments

  • Historical research by S.L.A. Marshall found that only 15-20% of soldiers in combat actively engaged, illustrating diffusion of responsibility in groups.
  • The 80/20 principle recurs in organizational settings, where a minority of people do most of the work.
  • Modern tech tools aimed at enhancing collaboration often create excessive coordination overhead without increasing actual output.
  • Collaboration can obscure individual accountability, making failure a collective issue rather than an individual one.
  • Research by Ringelmann and Brooks shows that group size and added personnel can reduce individual effort and increase communication overhead.
  • High-quality work is often done by individuals or small groups with clear authority and accountability, not large collaborative teams.
  • The culture of collaboration can discourage decisive individual action and ownership, which are necessary to complete work effectively.
  • The article calls for a return to trusting individuals with responsibility and accountability rather than relying on extensive collaborative processes.

Truthfulness Assessment

Opinion

The article accurately references well-documented phenomena such as the diffusion of responsibility, the Ringelmann effect, and Brooks' Law, all supported by historical and organizational research. The claim that collaboration tools can increase coordination overhead is consistent with studies on workplace productivity. However, the article presents a strongly opinionated critique without citing multiple sources or perspectives, and it generalizes about collaboration culture in a way that may not apply universally. The historical references to Marshall's combat research and IBM's feature usage are broadly accurate but simplified. Overall, the factual claims are grounded in recognized research, but the interpretation is subjective and somewhat one-sided.

Additional Considerations

  • The article does not consider the benefits of collaboration in fostering innovation, knowledge sharing, and employee engagement.
  • It overlooks contexts where collaboration is essential, such as complex problem-solving and creative industries.
  • The critique may not apply equally across all types of organizations or teams, especially those that successfully balance collaboration and individual accountability.
  • There is no discussion of how to effectively integrate collaboration and ownership rather than framing them as mutually exclusive.
  • The article does not address potential biases in the original studies cited, such as Marshall's methodology controversies.
