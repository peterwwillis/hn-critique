Summary

A large systematic review and meta-analysis published in The Lancet Psychiatry found no evidence that medicinal cannabis effectively treats anxiety, depression, or PTSD, and it may worsen mental health outcomes. The study also found limited and weak evidence for benefits in conditions like insomnia and autism, while highlighting potential risks such as psychosis and addiction.

Main Points & Arguments

  • Medicinal cannabis does not effectively treat anxiety, depression, or PTSD according to the largest review to date.
  • Cannabis use may increase risks of psychotic symptoms, cannabis use disorder, and delay more effective treatments.
  • Some limited evidence suggests potential benefits for conditions like insomnia, autism, epilepsy, multiple sclerosis spasticity, and certain types of pain, but this evidence is weak or low quality.
  • Cannabis-based treatments may help cannabis dependence but can worsen cravings in cocaine-use disorder.
  • The study calls for stronger regulation and oversight of medicinal cannabis due to rising use and uncertain safety and efficacy.
  • The findings are based on 54 randomized controlled trials conducted over 45 years and funded by the NHMRC.
