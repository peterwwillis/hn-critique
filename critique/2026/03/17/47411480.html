Summary

Scientists have discovered all the nucleobases that make up DNA and RNA in samples collected from the asteroid Ryugu, supporting theories that asteroids contributed to the origin of life on Earth. This finding aligns with previous detections of these molecules on asteroid Bennu and certain meteorites, suggesting these building blocks are widespread in the solar system.

Main Points & Arguments

  • Samples from asteroid Ryugu contain all five canonical nucleobases: adenine, guanine, cytosine, thymine, and uracil.
  • These nucleobases are essential components of DNA and RNA, the molecules underpinning life on Earth.
  • The discovery supports the hypothesis that carbonaceous asteroids contributed to the prebiotic chemical inventory of early Earth.
  • Similar organic molecules were previously found on asteroid Bennu and in meteorites such as Orgueil and Murchison.
  • A unique correlation was found between nucleobase ratios and ammonia concentration, suggesting a new pathway for nucleobase formation in early solar system materials.
  • Researchers emphasize that the presence of these molecules does not mean life existed on Ryugu, but that primitive asteroids could produce and preserve life-related chemistry.
