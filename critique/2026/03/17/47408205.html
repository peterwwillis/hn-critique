Summary

The article argues that each additional layer of review in a process slows progress by roughly tenfold, primarily due to waiting time rather than effort. It discusses the limitations of AI in overcoming these delays and emphasizes the importance of trust, quality culture, and system design to improve efficiency rather than simply adding more review layers.

Main Points & Arguments

  • Every layer of approval or review increases the process time by about 10 times, mostly due to waiting.
  • Doubling team size does not double speed because of coordination overhead and organizational design issues.
  • AI accelerates initial coding but does not significantly reduce review time, which remains a bottleneck.
  • Excessive review layers can hide root causes of defects and reduce overall quality by creating perverse incentives.
  • Deming's quality management principles suggest eliminating separate QA phases in favor of empowering workers to stop production when defects are found.
  • Trust among team members and management is crucial to creating a culture of quality and reducing the need for multiple review layers.
  • Smaller teams with well-defined interfaces and fewer review layers can produce higher quality work faster.
  • The article advocates for replacing traditional review layers with systemic quality improvements and modular design.
