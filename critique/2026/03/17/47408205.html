Summary

The article argues that each additional layer of review in a process increases the time to completion by roughly tenfold, primarily due to waiting and coordination overhead rather than effort. It critiques traditional quality assurance methods and suggests that reducing review layers, combined with trust and systemic quality improvements, is necessary to improve speed and quality in software development.

Main Points & Arguments

  • Every additional layer of approval or review increases process time by about 10 times, mostly due to waiting.
  • AI coding accelerates initial code creation but does not significantly reduce overall review time or process latency.
  • Traditional QA approaches, involving multiple review layers, can reduce quality due to misaligned incentives and reliance on catching errors late.
  • Deming's quality philosophy, emphasizing trust and systemic quality improvements rather than layered inspections, offers a better model.
  • Reducing review layers without replacing them with better quality systems risks introducing defects and failures.
  • Smaller, trusted teams producing high-quality components with clear interfaces can improve speed and quality.
  • The article suggests a future where modular, small-team development combined with AI can optimize quality and speed, but trust and systemic change are essential.
