Summary

The article proposes classifying happiness as a psychiatric disorder, termed 'major affective disorder, pleasant type.' It argues that happiness is statistically abnormal, involves a distinct cluster of symptoms, is linked to cognitive abnormalities, and may reflect abnormal central nervous system functioning.

Main Points & Arguments

  • Happiness is statistically abnormal compared to typical mental states.
  • Happiness consists of a discrete cluster of symptoms similar to psychiatric conditions.
  • Happiness is associated with cognitive abnormalities.
  • Happiness may reflect abnormal functioning of the central nervous system.
  • The common objection that happiness is not negatively valued is dismissed as scientifically irrelevant.

Truthfulness Assessment

Questionable

The article presents a highly unconventional and controversial hypothesis that happiness should be classified as a psychiatric disorder. This view is not supported by mainstream psychiatric or psychological consensus, which generally considers happiness a positive mental state rather than a disorder. The claim that happiness is statistically abnormal and associated with cognitive abnormalities lacks broad empirical support and contradicts extensive research on subjective well-being. The article is a provocative theoretical proposal rather than an evidence-based conclusion and does not reflect accepted diagnostic criteria or clinical practice.

Additional Considerations

  • Happiness is widely regarded as a positive and desirable emotional state in psychological research and clinical practice.
  • Current major diagnostic manuals such as the DSM-5 and ICD-11 do not classify happiness or positive affect as disorders.
  • The article does not address the social, cultural, and ethical implications of pathologizing happiness.
  • The proposal ignores extensive research on the benefits of happiness for mental and physical health.
  • The dismissal of the objection that happiness is not negatively valued overlooks the importance of subjective well-being in mental health.
