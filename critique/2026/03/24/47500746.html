Debunking Zswap and Zram Myths (chrisdown.name)

Summary

The article explains the differences between Linux's compressed swap technologies zswap and zram, recommending zswap for most use cases due to its tighter integration with the kernel's memory management and automatic tiering. It highlights zram's limitations, such as lack of automatic eviction and potential performance issues, making it suitable mainly for embedded or diskless systems with specific constraints.

Main Points & Arguments

  • zswap acts as a compression layer in front of disk swap, automatically evicting cold pages to disk and integrating with the kernel's memory management.
  • zram creates a compressed block device in RAM with a fixed capacity and no automatic eviction, which can lead to LRU inversion and system hangs under pressure.
  • Using zram alongside disk swap can degrade performance by filling fast RAM with cold pages and pushing active pages to slow disk.
  • zswap handles incompressible data better by rejecting it and sending it directly to disk, while zram compresses all data regardless of compressibility.
  • zswap reduces SSD wear by absorbing frequent page-out/page-in operations in RAM, whereas zram can increase disk I/O by forcing more page cache eviction.
  • Fedora uses a zram-only approach with systemd-oomd to avoid disk swap for security and complexity reasons, but this setup requires careful management to avoid system hangs.
  • Future kernel development favors zswap as the single compressed swap implementation, potentially with backing-store-less operation, reducing the need for zram.
