Summary

Bill C-22, the Lawful Access Act introduced in Canada, revises previous lawful access legislation by limiting warrantless access to subscriber information from telecommunications providers while expanding surveillance and monitoring obligations for electronic service providers. The bill introduces new metadata retention requirements and mandates cooperation from network and electronic service providers, raising significant privacy and civil liberties concerns.

Main Points & Arguments

  • Bill C-22 replaces the broader warrantless access provisions initially proposed in Bill C-2 with a narrower 'confirmation of service' demand power limited to telecom providers.
  • Access to subscriber information beyond service confirmation now requires judicially approved production orders.
  • The Supporting Authorized Access to Information Act (SAAIA) within Bill C-22 mandates electronic service providers to assist law enforcement with surveillance capabilities, including testing and data access.
  • The bill introduces metadata retention obligations for core providers, requiring retention of transmission data for up to one year, but excludes content, web browsing history, and social media activities.
  • There are concerns about security vulnerabilities, secrecy, cross-border data sharing, and insufficient oversight despite the involvement of the Intelligence Commissioner.
  • The bill's definition of electronic service providers extends beyond telecom to include internet platforms like Google and Meta, potentially broadening surveillance scope.
