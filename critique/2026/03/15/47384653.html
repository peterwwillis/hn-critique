Main Points & Arguments

  • Hobbes compares the commonwealth to an artificial man, with sovereignty as its soul and laws as its reason.
  • He argues that humans are driven by passions and desires, leading to a natural state of war without a common authority.
  • The social contract involves individuals surrendering some freedoms to an absolute sovereign to ensure security and order.
  • The sovereign's power is absolute and indivisible, necessary to prevent civil war and maintain peace.
  • Hobbes discusses the role of religion, interpreting scripture to support civil authority and criticizing ecclesiastical power that undermines sovereignty.
  • He emphasizes obedience to the sovereign as essential, except in rare cases such as the sovereign's failure to protect subjects.
  • The text critiques traditional philosophical and religious doctrines, advocating for reason and a strong civil authority.
