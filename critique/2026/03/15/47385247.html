The Serial Safety Net: Efficient Concurrency Control on Modern Hardware (muratbuffalo.blogspot.com)

Summary

The article discusses the Serial Safety Net (SSN), a concurrency control mechanism designed to achieve serializability in database systems running on modern hardware. SSN operates as a certifier on top of weaker isolation levels like Read Committed (RC) or Snapshot Isolation (SI), preventing dependency cycles and ensuring serializable transaction histories without the performance penalties of traditional methods like Two-Phase Locking (2PL).

Main Points & Arguments

  • Modern hardware with many cores and large memory challenges traditional concurrency control methods like 2PL due to contention.
  • Weaker isolation levels such as RC and SI offer better performance but allow anomalies and dependency cycles.
  • SSN is introduced as a certifier that tracks transaction dependencies and aborts transactions that would create cycles, guaranteeing serializability.
  • SSN uses two watermarks per transaction to detect cycles and may abort transactions conservatively to prevent future cycles.
  • Compared to standard Optimistic Concurrency Control (OCC), SSN avoids repeated aborts by ensuring safe retry conditions.
  • SSN requires additional metadata storage per tuple and does not natively handle phantom reads, necessitating supplementary mechanisms.
  • Layering SSN on SI provides a consistent snapshot view for applications, avoiding mid-transaction anomalies seen with RC+SSN despite both achieving serializability.
