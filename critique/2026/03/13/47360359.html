Summary

The article reviews Alison Bashford's book 'Decoding the Hand,' which traces the history and cultural significance of hand-reading practices such as palmistry and chirology from ancient times to the modern era. It explores how interpretations of the hand have evolved from mystical and religious frameworks to scientific and psychological understandings, while also discussing the persistence of hand-reading in contemporary culture.

Main Points & Arguments

  • Physiognomy and phrenology historically sought to infer character and mental states from physical features, especially the face and skull.
  • Hand-reading, including palmistry and chirology, has been practiced across many cultures and linked to divination, medicine, and psychology.
  • Religious and legal authorities historically condemned fortune-telling practices, associating them with foreign influences and fraud.
  • In the 19th and 20th centuries, hand-reading evolved toward more scientific and psychological interpretations, with some practitioners gaining scholarly recognition.
  • Fingerprinting emerged as a reliable forensic tool distinct from palmistry, focusing on individual identity rather than personality or fate.
  • Modern genetics and brain imaging have largely replaced traditional hand-reading as means to understand human traits and health risks.
  • Despite scientific advances, palmistry and related practices persist today, often combined with other psychic services and appealing to personal insight rather than predictive certainty.
