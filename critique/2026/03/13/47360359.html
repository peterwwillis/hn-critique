Summary

The article reviews Alison Bashford's book 'Decoding the Hand,' which explores the history and cultural significance of hand-reading practices such as palmistry and chirology from ancient times to the present. It discusses how hand-reading has been linked to science, medicine, magic, and identity, and how modern genetics and technology have shifted the understanding of human identity from surface readings to genetic and biometric data.

Main Points & Arguments

  • Physiognomy and phrenology historically attempted to infer character and mental traits from physical features, especially the face and skull.
  • Hand-reading (chirology and palmistry) has been practiced across various cultures and linked to divination, medicine, and psychology.
  • Religious and legal authorities historically opposed palmistry and fortune-telling, associating them with foreign influences and fraud.
  • In the 19th and 20th centuries, palmistry evolved from mystical to more scientific and psychological interpretations, though it remained controversial.
  • Fingerprinting emerged as a reliable forensic method distinct from palmistry, used for individual identification rather than personality insight.
  • Modern genetics and brain imaging have largely replaced traditional surface readings with biological and neurological understandings of identity and behavior.
  • Despite scientific discrediting, palmistry and related practices persist today, often combined with other psychic services and sought for personal insight rather than predictive certainty.
