Summary

The article discusses the impact of large language model (LLM) coding assistants on developers, highlighting a divide between those who value the craft of coding and those focused on results. It explores the concept of alienation from the act of coding, drawing on Marxist theory, and argues that the root issue lies in market and social structures rather than the technology itself.

Main Points & Arguments

  • There are two camps of developers: craft-lovers who value the process of coding and make-it-go people who focus on outcomes.
  • LLM coding assistants reveal an existing divide by enabling faster output but bypassing the creative act valued by craft-lovers.
  • Marx's concept of alienated labor is used to explain the separation from the act of working caused by reliance on LLM tools.
  • The market penalizes developers who do not use LLM assistants because productivity metrics favor speed and output.
  • The alienation is not caused by the technology itself but by the capitalist structure that ties livelihood to productivity metrics.
  • In non-capitalist or differently structured environments, LLM tools could be liberating rather than alienating.
  • The author’s personal experience with public funding allows selective use of LLM tools, preserving the craft aspect of coding.
