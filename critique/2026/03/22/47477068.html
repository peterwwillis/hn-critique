Convincing Is Not Persuading (blog.alaindichiappari.dev)

7 points by alainrk 1 hour ago  | 5 comments critique  | original on HN

Summary

The article distinguishes between convincing and persuading, emphasizing that while convincing relies on logical arguments aimed at a universal rational audience, persuading targets specific audiences by addressing their emotions, context, and incentives. It argues that technical proposals often fail because they focus solely on logical correctness without engaging the full human factors necessary for persuasion.

Main Points & Arguments

  • Convincing is based on logical validity and aims at a universal rational audience.
  • Persuading targets a specific audience's emotions, context, and motivations to drive action.
  • Technical proposals often fail because they focus on convincing rather than persuading.
  • Effective persuasion requires combining logic, credibility, and emotional understanding.
  • Authority alone can enforce compliance but does not guarantee genuine commitment or successful change.

Truthfulness Assessment

Opinion

The article accurately reflects established rhetorical theory, particularly the distinction between convincing and persuading as discussed by scholars like Perelman and classical rhetoric principles involving logos, ethos, and pathos. The practical observations about engineering and organizational decision-making align with common experiences reported in management and communication literature. However, the article is primarily an opinion piece based on the author's interpretation and experience rather than a presentation of new empirical research or multiple sourced perspectives.

Additional Considerations

  • The article does not cite specific empirical studies or multiple sources to support its claims.
  • It generalizes about engineers and decision-makers without acknowledging variability across organizations and cultures.
  • The role of organizational culture and structural factors in decision-making is not deeply explored.
  • The article assumes persuasion is always preferable to convincing, which may not apply in all contexts.
  • It does not address potential ethical concerns around persuasion techniques.
