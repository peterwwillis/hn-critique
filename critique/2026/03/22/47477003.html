Turns out your coffee addiction may be doing your brain a favor (theregister.com)

48 points by Bender 4 hours ago  | 30 comments critique  | original on HN

Summary

A long-term study suggests that moderate caffeine consumption, roughly two to three cups of coffee or tea daily, is associated with an 18% lower risk of developing dementia and better cognitive performance. The findings are observational and do not establish causation but highlight potential brain health benefits of steady caffeine intake over decades.

Main Points & Arguments

  • Researchers tracked over 130,000 people for more than 40 years, finding an association between moderate caffeine intake and reduced dementia risk.
  • Participants consuming moderate caffeine had better cognitive test scores and fewer memory complaints compared to those with low caffeine intake.
  • The benefits were linked to consistent, mid-range caffeine consumption rather than high doses.
  • The study used data from the Nurses' Health Study and Health Professionals Follow-Up Study, with 11,033 dementia cases recorded.
  • Researchers caution that the study is observational and cannot prove caffeine causes the reduced risk.
  • Possible mechanisms include improved blood flow, reduced inflammation, and effects on brain signaling.
  • Other lifestyle or socioeconomic factors may also contribute to the observed benefits.
