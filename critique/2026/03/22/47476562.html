Summary

The article discusses seven common mistakes to avoid when creating system architecture diagrams, emphasizing clarity and accuracy in representing system components and their relationships. It highlights issues such as unlabeled resources, disconnected elements, overly complex 'master' diagrams, oversimplified behavioral diagrams, distracting animations, fan traps, and overreliance on AI-generated diagrams.

Main Points & Arguments

  • Resources in diagrams should be labeled with both their type and specific names to avoid confusion.
  • All resources should be connected to others to clearly show their role and relationships within the system.
  • Creating a single 'master' diagram that attempts to show an entire system at once often overwhelms viewers and should be broken into multiple perspectives.
  • Behavioral diagrams should accurately represent interactions, avoiding oversimplification like the 'conveyor belt syndrome' that misleads viewers.
  • Meaningless animations in diagrams add no informational value and can distract from the technical content.
  • Fan traps occur when relationships between resources are lost due to collapsing relations onto intermediate resources, which can be fixed by adding more specific intermediate elements.
  • AI-generated system diagrams from source code are currently unreliable due to vague outputs, hallucinations, and lack of strategic inclusion of details.
