Main Points & Arguments

  • In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, London's electricity supply was fragmented with many small, incompatible systems using different voltages and frequencies.
  • Alternating current (AC) technology became the backbone of modern electricity systems due to its efficiency in long-distance transmission, despite initial resistance from proponents of direct current (DC).
  • The First World War exposed the inefficiencies of the fragmented system, leading to government encouragement of interconnection and moves toward a coordinated national grid.
  • The Electricity (Supply) Act 1919 created regional joint electricity authorities, but without enforcement powers, limiting progress.
  • The Central Electricity Board, established by the Electricity Supply Act 1926, created a synchronized AC grid starting operations in 1933, known as the National Grid.
  • Post-World War II nationalization under the Electricity Act 1947 consolidated generation and transmission, improving coordination and investment.
  • The Supergrid was built between 1950 and 1960 to connect regional grids at higher voltages, enhancing capacity and efficiency.
  • Privatization in the late 20th century reintroduced private ownership and competition, with mixed outcomes on investment and pricing.
  • In 2024, the UK government re-nationalized the operation of the electricity network, moving towards national operation with private ownership.
