Summary

Researchers have analyzed battle damage on Pompeii's northern walls and propose that some of the marks were caused by an ancient repeating catapult called a polybolos, likened to an early form of a machine gun. This theory is supported by 3D modeling of the damage, comparison with ancient Greek engineering texts, and matching projectiles from Roman military sites.

Main Points & Arguments

  • Pompeii's northern walls show battle damage from the Siege of Sulla, including large circular craters and smaller four-sided holes.
  • The smaller holes, previously attributed to wear or general damage, may have been caused by a polybolos, a repeating catapult capable of rapid fire.
  • Researchers used laser scanning and photogrammetry to create 3D models of the damage and inferred the weapon type from the shape and pattern of impacts.
  • Comparison with third-century BC Greek engineering blueprints supports the hypothesis of a repeating catapult.
  • Matching projectiles from other Roman military sites align with the dimensions of the modeled impacts.
  • The damage is well preserved due to Pompeii's burial by volcanic ash less than a century after the siege.
