Summary

The article argues against the idea of a widespread bespoke software revolution, asserting that while custom software has existed for years, it often results in bloated and confusing products. The author believes most people prefer to use technology that simply works rather than build or maintain their own custom systems, even with AI tools becoming more accessible.

Main Points & Arguments

  • Bespoke software has existed for a long time but often suffers from poor quality and complexity.
  • Software creators are naturally enthusiastic about bespoke software, but most users are not interested in building their own tools.
  • Most people tolerate technology rather than embrace it and prefer solutions that reduce their workload without adding new responsibilities.
  • AI tools will be used by many, but only a small subset of people with prior interest in software development will build custom systems.
  • Access to software building tools does not equate to everyone becoming a software builder.

Truthfulness Assessment

Opinion

The article's claims align with common industry observations that bespoke software projects can be challenging and often result in suboptimal products. It correctly notes that most users prefer ready-made solutions rather than engaging in software development themselves. The skepticism about a mass bespoke software revolution driven by AI is reasonable, given current adoption patterns and the complexity of software development. However, the article is largely opinion-based and does not cite external sources or data to support its claims.

Additional Considerations

  • The article does not discuss recent advances in low-code/no-code platforms that are making custom software development more accessible to non-developers.
  • It overlooks potential future shifts in user behavior as AI tools become more integrated and user-friendly.
  • The perspective is primarily from a software industry insider and may underrepresent the needs and capabilities of certain user groups.
  • The article does not address the role of professional developers in supporting bespoke software for businesses that require it.
