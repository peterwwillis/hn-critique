Microservices and the First Law of Distributed Objects (2014) (martinfowler.com)

Summary

The article discusses Martin Fowler's 'First Law of Distributed Object Design,' which advises against distributing objects due to the complexity and failure risks of remote calls. Fowler explains that microservices do not violate this law because they avoid in-process/remote transparency and instead use coarse-grained, document-based communication, though he acknowledges the complexity distribution adds and the ongoing debate about microservices versus monolithic architectures.

Main Points & Arguments

  • The First Law of Distributed Object Design states 'don't distribute your objects' because remote calls differ fundamentally from in-process calls in speed and reliability.
  • Distributed objects aimed for transparency between local and remote calls, which is flawed due to network unreliability and latency.
  • Microservices avoid this flaw by not attempting in-process/remote transparency and using coarse-grained APIs over HTTP or messaging.
  • Distribution adds complexity, especially in handling failures, consistency, and performance, making monolithic designs simpler by default.
  • Despite his reservations, Fowler supports microservices empirically because many teams, including Netflix and Amazon, have found success with them.
  • There is no definitive evidence favoring microservices over monoliths; both have successful examples, and the choice depends on context.
  • The article emphasizes clear communication of lessons learned to help readers make informed architectural decisions.
