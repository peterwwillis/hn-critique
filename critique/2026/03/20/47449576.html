Summary

NJIT physicists have analyzed nearly 30 years of solar acoustic data to identify the solar dynamo, the magnetic engine driving the Sun's 11-year cycle, operating near the tachocline about 200,000 kilometers below the Sun's surface. Their findings, published in Nature Scientific Reports, provide new observational evidence linking deep solar interior dynamics to surface magnetic activity and sunspot migration.

Main Points & Arguments

  • The solar dynamo responsible for the Sun's magnetic cycle operates near the tachocline, a boundary layer about 200,000 kilometers below the surface.
  • Researchers used nearly three decades of helioseismic data from NASA's SOHO, SDO satellites, and ground-based GONG to analyze internal solar sound waves and plasma flows.
  • The study shows migrating rotation bands in the solar interior form patterns that correlate with sunspot migration on the surface.
  • The tachocline's shearing flows are critical in generating the Sun's magnetic fields and driving the solar cycle.
  • Findings highlight the need to include deep solar interior layers, especially the tachocline, in space weather prediction models.
  • Understanding the solar dynamo can inform studies of magnetic activity in other stars with similar cycles.
